A distant reading of five issues of Enculturation

This project looked at five issues (23-27) from the scholarly journal Enculturation. I took text from articles and issue introductions, choosing to ignore book reviews and bibliographies. The text included available transcripts and image/figure descriptions when available on the same page. Using the text, I pasted each issue's collection into a free online word cloud generator TagCrowd. The parameters of the word cloud included: a limit of 50 most frequent terms, using stop words (digital, rhetoric, and rhetorical), and grouping similar words.

What did you see in these word clouds?

Enculturation focuses on contemporary takes on rhetoric, writing, and culture. Submissions should touch on rhetoric, composition, media, technology, and education and published articles should cover a broad range of topics that can relate to any of these subjects. Based on the journal's name and web description, I expected to see patterns related to the process of normalizing a culture's expectations through unconscious repetition. This is the idea that the parameters of a culture's norms and values inhibit social possibilities. Language and how we use it reflects, perpetuates, and normalizes how we think.

Each issue's word cloud suggests that there is an underlying theme that connects the articles within the journal. Issue 26's word cloud emphasized "antisemitism," while issue 27's word cloud emphasized "Asian American" and Issue 24's word cloud emphasized "disability." Despite the obvious differences between each issue's potential theme, every word cloud included words like "cultural," "public," "space," and "people." Seeing these words across the selection made me think about language its capacity to create space, engage with space, or cause limitations. This thought echoes back to Edbauer's (2009) focus on reimagining the rhetorical situation with an ecological focus; "rhetorical situations involve the amalgation and mixture of many different events and happenings that are not properly segmented into audience, text, or rhetorician...[they] are co-ordinating processes, moving across the same social field and within shared sturctures of feeling." Digital rhetoric, which I can't seem to classify as a subset of rhetoric because at it's base it's simply rhetoric, is pervasive in all interactions and influences how we interpret and engage with the world around us.

How has this distant reading helped you make sense of "digital rhetoric"?

If I go along with Eyman's (2015) simplified definition of digital rhetoric, it's basically rhetoric reframed to focus on digital as a means or mode. Coming into the course with minimal background in rhetoric studies and also because of my generation, I assumed digital meant post-Y2K—that this would be a course that focused on communication via computers, mobile technology, and the internet. And it did that, but not only that, as we were reminded by Haas' discussion of wampum belts as non-Western hypertexts and technologies (2007). Words like digital and technology are not limited to tools with screens; they've existed throughout the many centuries humanity as lived on this planet. Because I'm thinking about digital not as computers-only, I'm not sure that I can separate digital rhetoric from rhetoric.

The word clouds generated provide the top 50 most frequent terms of each issue in a context distanced from the original text. While word watching can provide terms to consider or patterns to notice, as someone new to the field it was like wading through a sea of unidentified objects with a muddled map to go on. I can look at the terms mentioned above and can create an idea based off of my bias, but it may not be an accurate representation of the journal.
