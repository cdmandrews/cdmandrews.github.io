Introduction

In this webtext, we (eight Master of Arts degree-seeking students and their professor) share our exploration of the word cloud as a digital rhetoric artifact, describing our attempts to engage word clouds as a distant practice of novitiate word-watching in the field of digital rhetoric.

Distant reading and network visualization have been offered up as potential entry points for newcomers to scholarly disciplines (Brooke, 2009; Mueller, 2017). One method of distant reading is through the construction of visual models such as tag clouds or word clouds, which can allow novices to try to make some sense of the foreign terrains built in various discourse communities.

The project students took on was to create word clouds of recent issues of scholarly journals related to digital rhetoric. After creating their individual distant readings, students reflected on, analyzed, and shared their clouds with the class (published as HTML pages as part of a larger profile/portfolio site project), inviting conversation both about what sorts of readings are possible through this medium as well as themes or patterns that provided a way into particular scholarly journals or a sense of topics and recent trends in digital rhetoric.

In this webtext, we support Mueller's notion that distant reading methods can be an engaging and useful way in to a text, domain, or field. At the same time the limitations of this assignment remind us of the inherent difficulty of entry points: that despite artifacts that can be relatively easy to create and work with, there is no quick, utilitarian ingress to complex terrains like academic disciplines.

This webtext presents a variety of materials:

  • A (sometimes very frank) discussion of our attempt to use distant reading and visualization methods (as deployed in writing studies) at the graduate level
  • Forty word clouds created from the five most recent issues of selected scholarly journals in digital rhetoric and humanities, rhetoric and technology, and computers and writing (using the TagCrowd word frequency visualization tool). Readers of the webtext are invited to browse these clouds and consider what they reveal or obscure about familiar publications.
  • Reflective and analytical commentary from members of the class and the instructor on methods, processes, and meanings of these word clouds

Though we initially thought our webtext would simply offer a teaching case about word clouds as a pedagogical strategy to help introduce graduate students to academic journals and fields via distant reading methods, this webtext also discusses the value in visualizing networks, which allow us to build our own connections and interests as graduate students. What the webtext ultimately grapples with are reasons for engaging in distant reading methods as graduate students, in particular as students in a generalist MA program.
