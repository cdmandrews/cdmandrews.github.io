A distant reading of five issues of XChanges

Findings

The data collected represents five issues of XChanges over a two-and-a-half year period with each issue, in its entirety, acting as a single data unit. The word clouds, generated using TagCrowd, provided three trends of information: reoccuring words amongst the issues, terms logically associated with digital rhetoric, and outliers associated with topic-specific articles. All data available in each issue, including the editor's introduction, author bios, and bibliographies, were included to assist in examining topic trends.

Of the five most cited words, as defined by TagCrowd, three occured twice within the five issue sample of XChanges: students (issues 12.2/13.1 and 13.2), writing (issues 12.2/13.1 and 13.2), and users (issues 14.1 and 12.1). The repetition suggests topics of interest within XChanges. The terms students and users may be used interchangeably or be used as subcategories of each other (e.g. users, specifically students; or students, who are users of technology), but this distant reading cannot reveal much in this area.

As expected, at least one of every most cited word per issue of XChanges relates to digital rhetoric: digital, reading, ethos, rhetorical, design, social, and feedback, amongst others. As XChanges publishes twice annually, the fall issue is dedicated to publications by undergraduate students, while the spring issue publishes interviews and research work from graduate students:

Our Fall issue each year features undergraduate research. Our Spring issue features graduate-student research. In each undergraduate issue, we publish theses and research projects of upper-level undergraduate students. Our graduate-student issues feature MA- and Ph.D.-level original research from emerging professionals in our focal fields.

Words that might be considered "outliers" in this project help to inform seeing the variability of XChanges' trends and special topics of interest for authors in each issue. The most significant outliers significant outliers were in two of the five issues. Issue 12.2/13.1's word clouds revealed meat, processed,and red as surprising focuses. Issue 11.2 highlighted Driscoll and James.

Future Considerations

This distant reading project utilized TagCrowd's word cloud capabilities to visualize the word trends, and points to "turns" in the journal's various issues. Using more data from the XChanges archives would assist in quantifying trends beyond identifying main and obvious terms like students, users, and writing. Of the data collected for this project, the author biographies and editor's introductions may be of the least concern. These two pieces of information may act as "noise," only making the most frequent terms in the issue even more frequent.

This project discovered the limitations of TagCrowd when dividing the collected articles into undergraduate and graduate work. The researcher wanted to see if trends were possible between the two categories of the given two-and-a-half year period. However, TagCrowd could not accomodate the massive amount of data imposed on it.

This distant reading visualizes how rapidly topics can shift in a journal over a short period of time. These visualizations might assist graduate students in thinking about how often trends arise or how short-lived they may be. XChanges provides a limited snapshot at past conversations, or topics of interest, to assist in determining future trends. These visualizations can also help students make sense of the journal's identity over the two-and-a-half year period, though this is complicated by oscillation between topics typified by undergraduate- and graduate-level work.
