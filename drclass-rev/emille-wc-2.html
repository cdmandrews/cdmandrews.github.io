    Word-watching commentary

    The second time through the distant reading project was a more collaborative effort. Each student still visualized five issues of their respective journals, but with added constraints in method: body-text only (no author’s biographies or bibliographies) and the addition of new stop words that seemed most obvious after reviewing everyone's word clouds (digital, rhetoric, and rhetorical). These constraints allowed clearer visualization of words associated beyond the often-times typical association with the terms digital and rhetoric.

    Despite these changes, the “new” word clouds did not change as drastically as expected. The most frequent words in each issue were:

    • Issue 11.2: Driscoll, Ethos, James, Audience, Science
    • Issue 12.1: Users, Writers, Technology, Design, Social
    • Issue 12.2/13.1: Blog, Students, Meat, Processed, Red
    • Issue 13.2: Feedback, Students, Participants, Writing, Games
    • Issue 14.1: Archiving, Participants, Websites, Communication, Information

    The addition of stop words slightly disrupted the initial patterns found in my first project, though not as much as I'd expected. This project did overall highlight the flexibility or variability in articles and research interests in fields related to digital rhetoric. I do not think this project has helped me make sense of the field digital rhetoric itself. We have discussed in class how the definition of rhetoric, and even more so digital rhetoric, is in constant flux. Rhetoric scholars themselves struggle with encompassing what the discipline is. As students, if our teachers/professors/experts-of-the-field are unsure of this definition, word clouds, as stand-ins for the work that experts—and in the case of XChanges, students and future members of the field—can provide insight to the future direction of the field.

    My first project attempt explored the reliability of word clouds, especially with such a small sample of XChanges and with my classmates’ word clouds. XChanges Issue 12.2/13.1’s most frequent words are drastically different from the others. Why? When looking at the word clouds, even the least frequently used words are similar, so what does this suggest about this issue? Word clouds visualize the most frequently used words, not necessarily the most useful. These clouds provide a preview of the article content, but alone, the words are not useful: meat, processed, red. Okay. What about them? A word’s usefulness is determined by its context and my word clouds are no different.

    Word associations are fascinating to me because of the images they conjure: either because I have been socialized to their meanings (as per a classmate’s example, mist and castles are associated with gothic texts) or out of my experiences. Words cannot act alone. By extension, our old and new word clouds do not make meaning alone. The meanings there were created based on the interactions of myself and my colleagues, but we can only communicate through some kind of common ground.
