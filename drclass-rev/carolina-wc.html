A distant reading of five issues of Hybrid Pedagogy

About word-watching...

Hybrid Pedagogy is an online journal that focuses on learning, teaching, and technology. Since it has a rolling submissions and publications system, I took six consecutive articles and considered them an issue. They were numbered 1 through 5, from oldest through most recent. Some key words in these initial clouds included:

  • Hybrid Pedagogy "Issue" 1: Articles from August 10, 2017 to February 13, 2018. Students is the most used word, followed by work, college, digital, and teachers. We can see the main topics of the journal reflected in the words.
  • Hybrid Pedagogy "Issue" 2: Articles from February 16, 2018 to April 10, 2018. Once again, student is the most used word, followed by learning, course, class, and digital. Although the words are different from those in issue 1, they still relate to the same area, education.
  • Hybrid Pedagogy "Issue" 3: Articles from April 12, 2018 to June 14, 2018. Again, the most used word is students, followed by learning, educator, designers, and teaching. The fact that designers is following after digital was trending could mean that there will be more focus given to the digital aspect of the journal.
  • Hybrid Pedagogy "Issue" 4: Articles from June 22, 2018 to November 13, 2018. Students is the most used word, followed by learning, education, teaching, and pedagogy. Here, we are back to the more "traditional" words, it mimics to a certain extend what we saw in issue 1.
  • Hybrid Pedagogy "Issue" 5: Articles from December 6, 2018 to September 12, 2019. The most used word is, again, students, followed by learning, course,design, and work. Two other words that appeared frequently were Cheryl and Chris, which is most likely due to the transcript of an interview.

The second time around, I used the same issues, and the results were fairly similar. I feel, however, that I had a different approach to it. This time I had a feeling that I knew a little bit more what I was doing, but I'm not sure if it had something to do with the fact that I already knew what the results would be like. The first time around I had no idea about what was going on, what the word clouds were supposed to show or how they could be used, so I just did the project mechanically by following the instructions and sharing the results. The second time, however, I already knew what I was looking for, what the results were going to look like and what they would mean for my particular journal. This made it easier to achieve.

Findings and patterns

As the name of the journal would suggest, the most used words in the articles were related to learning and instruction, with student being, by far, the most used in all five issues. Some noticeable trends across these five word clouds: learning became more frequent from issue one to issue five; class and classroom started to emerge in issue one and consolidated through issue five; course emerged in issue two but was not present in issue three and four, only to then make a comeback in issue five.

The fact that it was the second time around doing this exercise brought different questions, patterns and insights. In this case, since we created word clouds of 50 words, compared to the 100 that I had the first time I did this, there was more clarity in terms of which words were more frequently found. Having fewer words also made me think that maybe there would be more precision in the words, and more focus on the particulars of the clouds.
