      3. Try a user-centered-design strategy like Card Sorting (read a full description of Card Sorting at usability.gov) to get users to help you find alternative ways of organizing content. There are many videos about card sorting on YouTube, but this video from Intellavia is a short but instructive overview. The basic card sorting process is to:
        1. Chop up your content into cards (post it notes, note cards, or pieces of paper work great).
        2. Find a representative user (someone who fits your visitor profile).
        3. Ask them how they think the cards should be arranged.
        4. Take notes. Let your user do the talking. Let them rearrange it however it makes sense to them.
          1. Remember: You Are Not Your User.
        5. Repeat with another representative user if necessary.
      4. Don't forget about Redish's seven guidelines in Chapter 7. Some of these will be more relevant than others for this project.
        1. Give people only what they need.
        2. Cut! Cut! Cut! And cut again!
          1. (But also remember that some people really do want to read stuff!)
        3. Think "bite, snack, meal."
        4. Start with your key message.
        5. Layer information.
        6. Break down walls of words.
        7. Plan to share and engage through social media.
      5. Don't forget about the value of pencil and paper. Read up on low-fi paper prototyping.
