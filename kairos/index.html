A short guide for first-time webtext book reviewers

A key passage from our submission guidelines on what we're not looking for:

We’re not looking for a standard, text-based article written in a word processing program such as Microsoft Word or a similar program. We don't accept traditional print essays. Because the work we publish integrates rhetoric, design, and code to produce a scholarly argument that is instantiated in the use of media and design, we also have fairly specific technical requirements (e.g. we typically don't accept work produced in proprietary formats or systems—like iWeb or Wix). Please see the Kairos Style Guide for details.

The Kairos style guide reviews design and coding requirements that will help keep your webtext rhetorical, accessible, usable, and sustainable.

The most for many first-time webtext authors, our sustainability requirements represent a big learning curve:

  • We need to be able to archive everything we publish, so we cannot accept webtexts hosted on third-party sites like WIX and Weebly.
  • We no longer accept webtexts in WordPress, or those composed in Adobe MUSE.
  • We strongly encourage authors to use standard, non-proprietary formats (HTML 5, CSS, etc.) rather than Flash or other embedded proprietary media or template engines.

Resources for getting started with HTML5 & CSS: First-time authors should definitely check out the introductory tutorials and topics on Mozilla Developer Network. The "Introduction to HTML" is required reading!

Honestly, if you've never written for the web before, I recommend "Letting Go of the Words" by Ginny Redish.

Common technical tripups:

  1. Not using markup tags correctly (p, h1-h6, etc.), semantically instead of decoratively (or just incorrect use of tags).
  2. inline styles
  3. Need more structure (i.e. subheadings) or multimodality (i.e. pictures, media, stuff) to break up walls of words.

Sadly, there aren't many great free WYSIWYG editors out there these days. There are free (and good quality) text editors, however. You might find any one of the following useful:

  • brackets
  • BBedit
  • notepad++

explain the possibilities for this genre (not chapter-by-chapter summary)

Many of the drafts we see--in particular, reviews of edited collections--follow a typical pattern. An introductory home page, a series of pages devoted to individual sections, and a concluding page. Indeed, if you dig through the kairos review archives (be careful--there's some crazy ish in there!) you'll find many examples that do just that. These essentially take a book review manuscript written in a word processor and break it up into five or so sequential pages, with little to distinguish it as a webtext--i.e. something that tries to take advantage of the webtext as a medium that can do things differently than printed text on a page (or digital text on a pdf version).

These webtexts commonly trudge through the book a chapter at a time for the largest portion of the review. The weakness here is that often these drafts don't effectively describe the book as a whole or reflect patterns and relationships in the text beyond what's already been suggested in the book's table of contents. These relationships are valid, but the job of a book reviewer is to go beyond summarizing the contents of a book and say what's good (or not) about a text? What does it do? What does it add? What is absent? Who needs this book, and why? What can they do with it? Why do you HAVE TO READ THIS BOOK OMG?

The other weakness in the chapter trudge is that it lends itself to those sequential pages. The text might as well appear as a pdf! So, what are other possibilities for the genre?

Also, there's no rule that says your webtext must be all HTML pages--what about a video? a podcast or series of audioclips? an accessible infographic? a prezi? something we haven't seen yet? A simple single-page HTML shell would be enough to get those ready for publication. (Just don't forget to create transcripts or accessible text for everything!)

kooky examples and what works (note: need to refine, categorize by kinks and kooks.)

April Conway, The available means of persuasion: mapping a theory and pedagogy of multimodal public rhetoric

  • engages in the work of multimodal authoring--music, recomposition
  • easier to avoid the chapter-by-chapter because it's not an edited collection

Brian Hendrickson, Attuning to Ambience: A review of Ambient Rhetoric

  • again, a single author piece, so this is easier. Does include an outline of the book, but the review itself is focused on the big concepts and important ideas (and gives examples)
  • lots of visuals

Almjeld et al, The f-word: A decade of hidden feminism in kairos

  • single scroll design

Angela Moore, Shifting to the Performative: A review of Embodied Consciousness

  • uses video and text to perform the review
  • single section about contents--big summary rather than digging into each piece alone--tries to get a vision of the sections as wholes and the book as a whole rather than summarizing or abstracting each chapter.

Simpson & Stanovic, Review of Naming What We Know

  • does roughly follow section breakdown of the book, doesn't fall into chapter divisions too hard--uses larger ideas rather than chapters to break down webtext
  • map visualization (not perfect, but a mediation of the book)

Not to oversell the insanity, but here's an oldie: Nick Carbone, Socrates in the Labyrinth: David Kolb Re-Thinks Argument and Philosophy

  • does the whole hypertext where am I what's going on thing. It is a labyrinth.

Thomsen, Jessi. A Review of Digital Media and Contemporary Technogenesis

  • does the section-by-section breakdown, but goes for a webtext-y thing providing three different ways to experience the review

Hoermann, Jacquelyn E. Social Media in Disaster Response: How Experience Architects Can Build for Participation

  • essentially linear, but emphasizes visual and tries to enact something of social media by focusing on short text, hashtags, and pictures of peoples.

Burnside, Elkie. Writer/Designer

  • audio emphasis
  • genre/section bender, also sort of an interview piece
  • doesn't follow structure of the book
  • offers engaged critique through teaching

Sheffield, J. Understanding Social Media

  • one of a number of prezi form webtexts. Emphasis on visual, good example of doing visual with text section breakdown, oriented around themes and core ideas.

Chamberlain, Elizabeth F. Facebook and Philosophy

  • The "build a mockup of the interface when you're writing about that interface" thing is a bit played now, but this is a good example of texts that do that.

Jacobs, John Jay. Rhetorical Innovation: A Review of Understanding Rhetoric, A Graphic Guide to Writing

  • the rare textbook review

deWinter, Jennifer The Art of Video Games, Curated by Chris Melissinos: A Rhetorical Review of the Exhibition, the Book, and the People

  • what the heck, a video game?

Advice and scholarship about academic book reviews.

Piece of advice number one: Read some book reviews! Find some good ones, and do what they do.

Wendy Belcher, "How to write an Academic Book Review" (on the author's webpage; Belcher is the author of Writing Your Journal Article in Twelve Weeks: A Guide to Academic Publishing Success) https://wendybelcher.com/writing-advice/how-to-write-book-review/

  • An overview of why and how one might want to write a book review. Advice about reading to review and the "classic book review structure."
  • Particularly useful would be the section "Avoiding Five Common Pitfalls"
    1. Evaluate, don't just summarize.
    2. Don't use the TOC as the structuring principle for your reviews
    3. Judge the book by its intentions, not your own.
    4. Don't spend too much time on gaps. Mention them, but don't belabor.
    5. Don't quote too much.

"H-Net Book Reviews: Enhancing Scholarly Communication with Technology". Eileen L. McGrath, Winifred Fordham Metz, & John B. Rutledge. (2005). College & Research Libraries 66.1. Retrieved from https://crl.acrl.org/index.php/crl/article/view/15704/17150

  • Compares book reviews on H-Net (on H-South) and print reviews in The Journal of Southern History (JoSH).
In the humanities, book reviews often occupy a third or more of a journal's page. The reasons for writing reviews are myriad. Scholars often write reviews to participate in their discipline's dialogue. to keep their names before their peers, or simply to boost their number of publications. They read reviews to learn of new books as well as to see how their peers receive other scholars' ideas. (p. 8)
  • Show significant differences in academic status of reviewers: Grad students and junior faculty wrote most H-South reviews, while senior faculty wrote more than two-thirds of SoSH reviews. Asking if younger or more senior scholars would be more critical, this analysis shows that neither is true. Recognizing that most reviews tend to be positive (though with variations in degree of enthusiasm) assessment of quality in H-South and JoSH reviews was about the same.
  • E-Reviews are significantly longer than print reviews (average lengths of e-reviews was three times the average length of print).
  • E-reviews showed a marked tendency to detail the contents of a book chapter by chapter (in a few cases, evaluative comments were so oblique that the researchers had to read very closely to determine the reviewer's opinion). The convenience of e-reviews may be undermined by their length. If a book review is bland and lacks critical assessment of the book, it may be of limited usefulness.
Reading an e-review was very much like reading a synopsis or a linear rehearsal of the arguments, findings, and overall content of the book. In some cases, the evaluative function of a book review seemed subordinate to the reporting function. [...] Some readers will find these long, detailed, but essentially descriptive reviews valuable. A bibliographer with responsibility for a focused collection will find the H-Net type of review most helpful. [...] However, mere description falls short of the intellectual dialogue necessary for the education of the discipline. (17)
The chief advantage of reviews such as those examined on H-Net is that librarians and scholars can get a good sense of what the book being reviewed contains. The disadvantage is that reporting about content can replace a genuinely critical evaluation. (p. 17)
By convention, a book review is a terse, tightly constructed and condensed form of communication. (p. 17)

My take: When dealing with edited collections, it might be especially tempting to overview EVERYTHING. Stick to main themes of the book. Why is the work relevant now? What are the outstanding (or less outstanding) contributions in an edited collection?

Brienza, Casey. (March 2015). "Writing Academic Book Reviews" Inside Higher Ed #Career Advice. Retrieved from https://www.insidehighered.com/advice/2015/03/27/essay-writing-academic-book-reviews

  • Nothing especially surprising in her overview of book review structure, and it of course mostly speaks to the structure of print reviews, but a useful heuristic for thinking an evaluation through.
  • Brienza breaks down the traditional structure of academic book reviews: Introduction, Summary of argument, About the author(s), Summary of contents, Strength, Weakness, and Conclusion.
  • I would add academic context/contributions to the field to this list. You should be able to talk about how/where the work fits in the field and what contributions it is making.

Another, slightly different guide from USC Libraries research guides: https://libguides.usc.edu.

  • The guide discusses the purposes of a book review, and the heuristic for "Critical or essay-length book reviews" would be particularly useful.
The task of the book reviewer is to “tease out” the book’s themes, explain them in the review, and apply a well-argued judgment on the appropriateness of the book’s argument(s) to the existing scholarship in the field.
