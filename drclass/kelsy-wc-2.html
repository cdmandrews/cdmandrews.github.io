Peitho Word Clouds

For this word cloud creation project, I investigated the last five issues of the Peitho journal. These issues included Volume 19 Issue 2, Volume 20 Issue 1, Volume 20 Issue 2, Volume 21 Issue 1, and Volume 21 Issue 2. Peitho is a journal that explores ideas of feminism and how those concepts apply to pieces of literature, as well as writing and rhetoric. Each issue of Peitho can be downloaded in pdf form as either the whole issue as one large document or with each article downloaded separately. Since the journal focuses on concepts of feminism, I expected the highest count words to relate to the topic.

Methodology

In order to create the word clouds, I first downloaded the five Peitho issues as pdf documents. For Volume 20 Issue 2, and Volume 21 Issue 1, the articles could only be downloaded individually instead of as part of the larger pdf document, which meant I had to use a third-party application to combine the articles back into one pdf document. The third-party application that I chose to combine the pdfs back into one document was CombinePDF.com. Volume 19 Issue 2, Volume 20 Issue 1, and Volume 21 Issue 2 were all able to be downloaded in their entirety as one pdf document.

Initially, I used WordClouds.com to upload each pdf in its entirety to create flat jpg word cloud images for each issue. For the second round of word cloud creation, however, I copied and pasted only the content of each article into a rich-text file, with each issue only having one rich-text file. I opted to leave out any editor’s introductions, acknowledgements, and bibliographies in order to isolate only the content of each article. Additionally, I removed all page numbers and page headings from the copy-and-pasted texts so as to not skew the data with unnecessarily repeated words. I then uploaded the rich-text files individually into the word cloud creator TagCrowd.com, with the specifications that each cloud would have only 50 words, using the stop words “digital,” “rhetoric,” and “rhetorical.”

Description of Word Clouds

In the word cloud for Peithos Volume 19 Issue 2, the largest word within the word cloud is “women,” with the next largest being “work,” “home,” “feminist,” and “space.” In the cloud for Volume 20 Issue 1, the largest word is “students,” with the next largest being “women,” “young,” and “girls.” In the word cloud for Volume 20 Issue 2, the largest word was also “women,” with the next largest being “white” and “feminist.” The cloud for Volume 21 Issue 1 had the largest word being “women” with the next largest being “education” and “work.” In the last cloud, Volume 21 Issue 2, the largest words were “women” and “work” with the next largest being “chair” and “feminist.”

Data Analysis

By converting these five articles into word clouds, and thereby displaying these five issues in a nonlinear means of organization, the reader is able to have a better understanding of which topics are important to this journal, regardless of the theme of the individual issues. For example, in each of the five issues, the word “women” was the largest and most used word, meaning that the Peitho journal is concerned most of all with issues relating to women, which makes sense considering the journal investigates topics of feminism.

Additionally, by displaying these issues in the nonlinear organization style of the word clouds, with only the 50 most common words displayed in each of the five images, the reader is able to easily identify patterns and trends among the words. For example, aside from the most used word being “women,” another word that appears in all of the images is the word “feminist,” which also aligns with the fact the journal is a feminist publication. Yet another trend that appears among the images are the words that relate to the individual themes of each issue. For example, in Volume 19 Issue 2, the words “bodies,” “gender,” “queer,” space,” and “lady” appear within the top 50 most used words, suggesting that the theme for the issue is something having to do with LGBTQ identities and the spaces female bodies occupy. Volume 21 Issue 2, however, contains words such as “academic,” “institutional,” “faculty,” and “department” as some of the most used words, suggesting a theme of academics for that issue.

Organizing the issues into word clouds provides insight into the political advocacy discussions the Peitho journal is having among its writers and readers. By displaying the words “women,” “feminism,” “work,” “space,” and “writing,” as the most used words across the five issues, the word clouds show that the issues regarding the spaces women are occupying in their work and writing are some of the most important issues to this journal. Additionally, by looking at the smaller themes of “gender,” “shaming,” “refugees,” “histories,” and “accountability,” across the issues, the reader can assume the journal sets out within each issue to discuss a current political topic and women writers’ place within those topics.

Grappling with "Digital Rhetoric"

Overall, this word cloud activity has helped me make sense of the concept of “digital rhetoric” because it allowed me to work with digital texts in unique ways that I was previously unfamiliar with. According to Eyman (2015), digital rhetoric is both the study of the rhetorical strategies at work within a digital text, as well as how those rhetorical strategies can be applied to our own pieces of writing. Before working on this project, the only time I had used word clouds was to show my students the most common class response to a topic we were studying. Now, I recognize that word clouds can be used to gather a unique perspective on a body of work, with the nonlinear organization style serving to showcase the most common topics and issues within the journal. Through this activity, I realized that digital rhetoric is more than simply reading a text in an online environment. It can also consist of creating one’s own writings through digital means, or using digital tools to help analyze another digital text, as in the case of the word clouds and digital Peitho journal.
